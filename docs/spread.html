How truth spreads

You don't need everyone. You need enough.

3.5%
No nonviolent movement that mobilized 3.5% of the population has ever failed.
— Erica Chenoweth, Harvard, studying every movement from 1900–2006

You don't need to convince everyone. You don't need to convince the powerful. You don't need to convince the comfortable. You need to reach enough people who already feel what you feel but think they're alone.

They're not alone. They just can't see each other yet.

What spreads

Not data. Not reports. Not 86 indicators and pattern-matching scores. Those exist for verification. They're the foundation. But foundations don't spread. Stories spread. Feelings spread. One sentence that makes someone stop scrolling spreads.

Every truth that changed history had three properties:

1. Simple enough to repeat in one sentence

If you can't say it in one sentence, it won't travel. Not because people are stupid. Because attention is finite and the world is loud.

"He promised to fight corruption. His family got $2 billion."
That's one sentence. Anyone can repeat it. Anyone can check it.

2. Felt before understood

People don't share data. They share what they feel. One photo of a child's body on a beach changed European policy overnight. A thousand policy papers couldn't do it.

Lead with the human. The worker who lost their home. The child who lost their parent. The family who lost everything while the leader built a palace. Then show the data for anyone who wants to verify.

3. Impossible to deny

This is where evidence grading matters. Grade A — court records, financial disclosures, their own words on camera — can't be called fake news. It's their signature. Their filing. Their face.

The format that travels

Three lines. That's all you need.

Example — how to see clearly
THEY SAID: "I will fight for the working people."
THEY DID: Cut taxes for the richest. Workers' wages stayed flat.
Evidence: Grade A — Congressional voting record, IRS data, BLS wage statistics
Example
THEY SAID: "We care about the environment."
THEY DID: Internal memos from 1977 show they knew their product caused climate change. They funded denial for 40 years.
Evidence: Grade A — internal corporate documents, court discovery
Example
THEY SAID: "A short, special operation."
THEY DID: Hundreds of thousands dead. Cities flattened. Economy halved.
Evidence: Grade A — UN reports, satellite imagery, economic data

Three lines. Anyone can read them. Anyone can verify them. Anyone can share them. The format is the weapon.

How it grows

You tell 5 people

Not strangers. People who already feel it. Friends who rant at dinner about the same things you rant about. People who say "something is wrong" but don't know what to do. Give them the page. Give them the format.

Each of them tells 5

That's 25. Then 125. Then 625. That's how every real movement started. Not with a broadcast. With a conversation.

Someone with reach picks it up

A journalist. A teacher. A YouTuber. Someone with an audience who has been looking for exactly this: a format that makes the truth undeniable and shareable. You don't need to find them. They find things like this. That's their job.

It becomes a reflex

When enough people are used to asking "what did they say vs what did they do?", it stops being a tool and becomes a habit. A cultural norm. The question you ask before you vote, before you buy, before you trust. That's when it's won.

What about the people who won't look?

Leave them. Seriously. Leave them.

You will waste your entire life trying to convince someone who has decided not to see. That's not your job. Your job is to make the truth available, clear, and undeniable. Some people will look at a financial disclosure showing billions stolen and say "but he's strong." You can't fix that. That's not blindness. That's choice.

Focus on the ones who want to see but can't yet. The ones who feel something is wrong but don't have the words. The ones who would be furious if they knew. They're the 3.5%.

What about the powerful who will try to stop it?

They will try. They always do.

They'll call it fake. That's why evidence is graded — Grade A means it's their own documents.

They'll try to shut it down. That's why the code is open and anyone can run it. You can't shut down a language.

They'll try to flood it with noise. That's why every piece of evidence has a quality grade. Noise is Grade D. Signal is Grade A. You can see the difference.

They'll try to make it illegal. That's when you know it's working. Everything that scared a tyrant was illegal under that tyrant. Printing was illegal. Assembly was illegal. Speaking was illegal. The truth was always illegal to someone. It survived anyway.

· · ·

What can one person do?

One person can make one truth undeniable.

One SAID vs DID card with Grade A evidence. One sentence that someone shares at dinner. One record that a journalist picks up. One comparison that makes someone say "wait, what?"

That's enough. That's always been enough. Rosa Parks did one thing. One seat. One refusal. And it was undeniable, and it was felt, and it was simple enough to repeat in one sentence: "She was tired and she sat down and they arrested her."

You don't need to save the world. You need to make one truth so clear that it can't be ignored. Then make the next one. Then the next.
