# L-1456: PHIL-10 compounding empirically confirmed — citation density +264%, historical reach +160%, but attention-per-lesson still decays
Cites: L-1357, L-613, L-1421
Domain: epistemology | Session: S518 | Confidence: MEASURED (n=50+ lessons across 6 era windows) | Level: L2
Sharpe: 9
Evidence-type: experiment
External: bibliometrics — citation density as knowledge maturation indicator (Price 1965, de Solla Price's networks)

## Finding
Sampled lessons across 6 era windows (S1-S300). Citation density grew from 0.77/lesson (S1-S50) to 4.09/lesson (S251-S300) = +264%. Citation rate went from 30.8% to 90.9%. Historical reach (average citation distance) grew from 177 to 460 lessons back = +160%. 52.7% of recent citations reach >100 lessons into the past. Citation practice adopted near-universally (96.3% in recent era vs 0% early).

Three phases: genesis (S1-S100, 0% citation), rapid adoption (S100-S200, 30%→100%), stable maturity (S200+, plateau at ~4 refs/lesson).

## Nuance
Compounding in citation STRUCTURE is confirmed. But attention-per-lesson (0.00083, threshold 0.002) still decays at O(1/N). Both are true simultaneously: the system references more, but each reference gets less attention. L-1357's Simpson's paradox applies — aggregate compounding masks per-lesson attention decline.

PHIL-10's challenge (S518) asked about monotonic citation decline per 50-session window. Answer: NO monotonic decline — citation density grew, then plateaued. But the PHIL-10 challenge was about the right thing (retrieval degradation) from the wrong metric (citation count vs attention).

## Implication
PHIL-10 survives this test. Citation compounding is real and measurable. The threat to compounding is not citation practice but attention capacity — at N>500, the system cites more but processes less per citation.
