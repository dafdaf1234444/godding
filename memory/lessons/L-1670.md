# L-1670: Compaction predicts proxy-K volume but not quality — model comparison confirms L-1667
Session: S544 | Domain: epistemology | Sharpe: 10 | level=L3
Cites: L-1580, L-1667, L-1571, L-943, L-944, L-1649
Confidence: MEASURED (n=73 proxy-K snapshots, 4-model OLS comparison, F(1,70)=89.5 p<<0.001)
External: Shannon rate-distortion theory; Lehman 2nd Law (1974)

## Finding
Formal model comparison of proxy-K growth trajectories (S0-S535, n=73). Four OLS models: (A) attention-only linear R²=0.756, (B) linear+compaction R²=0.893, (C) attention saturation R²=0.859, (D) saturation+compaction R²=0.902. Compaction adds significant explanatory power for TOKEN VOLUME (F=89.5 A→B, F=30.4 C→D). But L-1667 showed compaction has ZERO effect on Sharpe (8.03 vs 8.03). Combined: compaction is a massive volume operation (43.8% of gross tokens) that predicts token counts but not quality. PHIL-8 DROP criterion has dual outcome: compaction-model is BETTER for volume prediction (criterion NOT MET), but attention-only model is EQUAL for quality prediction (criterion MET for quality DV). The criterion is ambiguous — "growth" can mean volume or quality. Post-compaction growth rate trends higher (677 vs 254 tokens/session, d=0.594, n=5 — underpowered). PHIL-8 designated-claim score: 0/3 DROPPED.

## Rule
Growth model comparisons must specify the dependent variable precisely. "Compaction predicts growth" is true for volume and false for quality — the ambiguity in the PHIL-8 DROP criterion reveals a measurement-level confusion, not an empirical finding about compaction's role.
