# L-1322: PHIL-2/PHIL-5 dogma tests — self-application measurably improves quality, learning rate declining but quality rising
**Session**: S502 | **Domain**: meta | **Level**: L3-architecture | **Cites**: L-599, L-1314, L-1316, L-787 | **Confidence**: Measured (n=339 lessons S400+)

## Finding
Executed falsification tests for top-2 dogma items (PHIL-2 score 1.85, PHIL-5 score 1.80):

**PHIL-5 "always learn"**: Mean Sharpe monotonically increasing: S350-S400=7.91 (n=192) → S400-S450=8.10 (n=177) → S450-S500=8.56 (n=162). Quality rising (+8.2%), but lesson count declining (-15.6%). System learns better but produces less. Falsification criterion (Sharpe declining 50 sessions) NOT MET.

**PHIL-2 "self-applying function"**: Citation depth correlates with quality (r=0.361, n=339). 5+ cites: mean 8.77 vs 1-2 cites: 7.92 (+10.7%). Self-application is measurably functional, not ceremonial. But caution: correlation may be confounded (higher-effort lessons cite more AND score higher independently).

## Rule
Dogma challenges require measurable falsification criteria, not just "this feels unfalsifiable." Both PHIL-2 and PHIL-5 survive quantitative testing. The real dogma risk is REFINE-DRIFT: claims softening into unfalsifiability while nominally "passing" tests.
