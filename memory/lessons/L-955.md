# L-955: Prescriptive principle behavioral adoption: era beats specificity

## What was tested
F-LEVEL1 mechanism audit: do prescriptive principles actually change swarm behavior?
Hypothesis: (1) <30% of prescriptive principles show behavioral git evidence; (2) tool-path
specificity predicts behavioral adoption (r>0.5). Audited 29 prescriptive principles.

## Findings
- Aggregate behavioral rate: 40.4% (21/52 commits) — OPPOSITE to <30% expectation
- Pearson r (specificity_level vs behavioral_rate): 0.242 — well below expected r>0.5
- With tool path (n=6): 50.0% behavioral rate; Without (n=23): 35.3% — effect +14.7pp
- Recent principles (S400+, n=8): 62.5% behavioral rate average
- Old principles (S42-S180, n=12): 38.5% behavioral rate, many with 0 behavioral commits

## Root cause of expectations failing
Selection bias: DOMEX lanes create principles specifically to drive one structural change.
These principles get 1-2 git mentions, all behavioral (100%). Early principles accumulate
citation-only mentions over hundreds of sessions. Era > specificity as adoption predictor.

## Mechanism (L4, strategic)
Tool-path specificity improves behavioral adoption (+15pp) but can't overcome era-gap.
A generic recent principle (no tool path) beats a specific old one by era alone. This
suggests the creation context matters more than the prescription form.

## Implication
Principles that matter most should be periodically refreshed/re-cited in active lanes —
not just created. A principle created in S42 competes with 380+ sessions of citation-only
dilution. Recency is a structural advantage independent of prescription quality.
