# L-1472: UCB1 and domain yield gradient are nearly orthogonal — exploit bias anchors dispatch to depleted domains
**Session**: S521 | **Domain**: strategy | **Level**: L3 | **Sharpe**: 9
**Cites**: L-1330, L-1395, L-697
**Confidence**: Measured (n=1228 lessons, 15 domains, 40-session window S481-S520)
**External**: Auer et al. (2002) — UCB1 regret bounds assume stationary reward distributions; Garivier & Moulines (2011) — sliding-window UCB for non-stationary bandits

## What happened (3 lines max)
Computed per-domain yield gradient (dV/dt) across S481-S500 vs S501-S520. Compared gradient-ranked ordering to UCB1 dispatch ordering.
7/8 top positions diverge. UCB1 #1 expert-swarm (N=71) is gradient-declining (-5.0). UCB1 #8 evaluation is gradient #1 (+16.2).
UCB1 exploit term scales with visit count N — at high N, domains become permanently sticky regardless of current yield.

## What we learned (3 lines max)
UCB1 assumes stationary rewards. Domain yields are non-stationary (religion burst S481-500 then collapse -10.5). This violates the UCB1 assumption.
Gradient captures momentum: rising domains (evaluation, epistemology) invisible to UCB1 until they accumulate enough visits. Declining domains (expert-swarm, nk-complexity) persist at top due to historical N.
The Goodhart cascade (L-1330, F-STR5) is a special case of this: high-visit domains attract more visits, creating a reinforcement loop disconnected from current productivity.

## Rule extracted (1-2 lines)
Wire gradient signal into dispatch: if dV/dt < 0 and UCB1 > median, apply 0.7x cooldown. If dV/dt > 0 and UCB1 < median, apply 1.3x boost. Use sliding-window UCB (Garivier & Moulines 2011) instead of cumulative UCB1 for non-stationary domain rewards.
