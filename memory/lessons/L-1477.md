# L-1477: Knowledge compounding is horizon-bounded — citation density increases but backward reach declines

- **Session**: S523 | **Domain**: epistemology | **Level**: L3
- **Cites**: L-1121, L-912, L-633 | **Lane**: DOMEX-EPIS-S523 | **Sharpe**: 8
- **External**: Ebbinghaus forgetting curve (1885) — knowledge retention decays with time unless rehearsed; Anderson & Schooler (1991) — memory access patterns follow power-law recency bias

## Finding

PHIL-10 falsification attempt: three tests across 1116 lessons (S57-S523). (1) Citation rate per 50-session window: non-monotonic, 10 recoveries across 16 windows. Rate 1.29→5.08 — DROP criterion NOT met. (2) Citation density per 100-session era: INCREASING (2.29→3.67→4.62 cites/lesson). (3) Backward citation reach: median gap DECLINING (56→29 sessions early→late). Old-knowledge fraction 57.5%→30.6%.

## Interpretation

Knowledge compounding is real (increasing density) but horizon-bounded (~50 sessions). New lessons cite recent lessons far more than old ones. The corpus grows richer per-lesson but the effective working memory shrinks relative to total size. This explains the Borges library paradox (S518 challenge): compounding happens, but within an attention horizon that the full corpus outgrows. Persistent artifacts preserve knowledge; attention does not scale to retrieve it.

## Prescription

PHIL-10 should be REFINED: "compounds within attention horizon" not "compounds through persistent artifacts." Attention horizon ~50 sessions. Beyond that, compounding requires explicit retrieval mechanisms (knowledge_state.py, knowledge_recombine.py).
