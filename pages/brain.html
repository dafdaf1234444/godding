godding / brain

brain · the stack · individual and collective

brain — each one is a finite stack.

Treat a brain as a stack. Slots are limited. At its best, a brain takes a topic in and pops out a small ordered set of responses — its own well-ordering on that topic. Civilisation is the same operation at scale, with extra moves.

the picture

the seven principles

principle 1 · finite stack a brain has a fixed cognitive budget. attention, working memory, and long-term recall are all bounded. treating the brain as if it were infinite is the root error behind almost every information-overload failure mode.
principle 2 · well-ordered response given a topic, a brain at its best returns an ordered set: most-likely answer first, second-best next, and so on. mathematically: a partial well-ordering of admissible responses, where the order is built from training data, recent context, and the body's current state. losing the order is what we call confusion.
principle 3 · compression keeps the stack from overflowing humanity's trick is to push state out of one brain and into shared structures: language, writing, schools, libraries, the internet, this website. each compresses many earlier brains' work into a form a single brain can replay cheaply. this is why religion, cross-cultural moral law, and dependency-tracked notes all work — they're external stack extensions.
principle 4 · some slots must stay frozen not every part of the stack is meant to move. breath, heartbeat, hunger, fear of falling, attachment to your child — these are slots evolution glued shut. trying to overwrite them is how addictions, eating disorders, and self-harm work. the rule: re-order anything except the frozen base.
principle 5 · cooperative stacks compound two brains that share their ordered sets can together produce a larger ordered set than either alone — that's thought as a team sport. the more cooperative the stack, the more society's collective ranking improves. play /good-bad for the smallest case: cooperators that cluster outlast lone defectors at every long timescale.
principle 6 · the more you change, the more you forget capacity is conserved. learn one thing, lose a slot. this is why deliberate stack-management matters: a person who reorders slowly and keeps the right things frozen ends up with deeper expertise than someone whose stack is in constant flux. that's also why doom-scrolling burns smart adults — it overwrites slots that took a decade to build.
principle 7 · some stacks are bad-ordered a small minority of brains rank cruelty, deceit, or domination above cooperation — not because of an empty stack, but because their order puts harm at the top. whether that's a disease, a deep injury, or an active choice is the open question of the crime and justice pages. the practical move is the same regardless: record what they do, take action where the evidence allows, and never assume their stack will fix itself in private.

what this means for godding

this whole site is a compression channel

godding's job is to tighten the universal short list (crime), expose the load-bearing systems (world), keep public records (justice), and let the swarm prune weak claims (swarm). all of it is collective-stack management.

references

  • Daniel Kahneman, Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011) — the original two-stack framing in plain language.
  • Karl Friston — free-energy principle — formal version of "predict, store the surprises".
  • Cesar Hidalgo, Why Information Grows (2015) — a clean version of compression-as-civilisation.
  • James Gleick, The Information (2011) — long-form history of how humans externalise stacks.
  • Edsger Dijkstra (1968), "The Humble Programmer" — the original "human stack is small, design accordingly" argument.
  • Norbert Wiener, The Human Use of Human Beings (1950) — what happens when machines extend the stack faster than the rules of cooperation update.
