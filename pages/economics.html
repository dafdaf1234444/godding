godding / economics

price systems · what a market actually measures

economics — price what costs the world, not what crowds will pay.

most prices today track willingness to pay. that's a useful signal, but it's not the only one. willingness ignores how much energy a thing took to make, how useful it actually is once made, how much carbon it leaves behind, and which dependencies it dragged along. this page sketches a layered alternative: an energy-numeraire currency on top of money, a usefulness metric on top of that, and a per-item carbon & dependency ledger shipped with every object. the goal isn't to replace markets; it's to give them more honest inputs.

why prices fail to track the real cost

  • externalities are off-balance-sheet. CO₂ doesn't show on a sales receipt. neither does river damage, antibiotic resistance, or political instability. classical microecon called these out in the 1920s (Pigou) and we still haven't priced them.
  • willingness ≠ value. a 60-second video can earn more than a year of farming. that says something about attention, not about which one you'd miss if it stopped.
  • no dependency view. a "$3 t-shirt" silently drags a cotton field, a dye plant, a container ship, and a landfill bin behind it. the price doesn't know.
  • the unit is arbitrary. fiat money is whatever the central bank says it is. it's a useful coordination signal, not a fundamental measure of how much world a thing costs.

the four layers — read them in order

1 · energy-numeraire currency (kWh-coin)

peg a parallel currency to the kilowatt-hour. one unit = one delivered kWh of low-carbon electricity, audited at a registry of meters and time-stamped on a public ledger. prices in kWh-coin change when the energy mix changes — which is the point. it isn't a replacement for fiat; it's a parallel scale that makes energy-cost legible inside any transaction.

2 · usefulness metric (joule-per-utility)

for every product class, define a service unit (one passenger-km, one calorie of actual nutrition, one hour of useful compute). divide its lifecycle energy by service units delivered. a sedan vs. a bicycle on the same trip have very different joule-per-passenger-km values. publish the ratio. let buyers see it on the shelf.

3 · per-item carbon label (kgCO₂e shipped with the object)

every item carries an embedded-carbon number computed under ISO 14067 / GHG Protocol Product Standard, attested by a third party, written into a QR code or RFID. supermarkets already do this for some lines (Oatly, Quorn, Unilever pilots); make it the default rather than the exception. the methodology is not new — scaling it is.

4 · dependency-tracked supply chain

every item ships with a small, signed graph of the inputs it depended on: where the metal was mined, which farm, which port, which factory. the consumer sees the graph the way they see a nutrition label. systems that already do this exist (EU Digital Product Passport from 2027, Provenance, IBM Food Trust). give them teeth.

what the math looks like

this isn't speculative — every variable has a real measurement procedure. here are the load-bearing definitions:

energy-cost(item) = Σ (joules consumed at each step in the supply chain)
carbon-cost(item) = Σ (kgCO₂e at each step, scope 1 + 2 + 3, GHG Protocol)
usefulness(item) = service-units delivered over expected lifetime
true-cost-ratio = (energy-cost + price-of-carbon × carbon-cost) / usefulness

none of those terms are new. each one has decades of methodology: life-cycle assessment (ISO 14040, since 1997), energy return on investment (Hall, 1981; Murphy 2014), service-unit accounting (the functional unit in LCA), and shadow price of carbon (UK Treasury Green Book; US EPA SCC). the work isn't inventing them — the work is publishing them on every item, on every receipt, in a unit a person can read.

the dependency graph that ships with each thing

every item is the leaf of a tree of upstream items. you cannot see the tree on a price tag. the proposal is the same one this site uses for its own pages (see /depends): each item declares what it rests on, signed and timestamped, and a checker rejects any inconsistency. the model is borrowed from how mathematicians ship proofs.

nodekinddeclared inputsdeclared outputs
cotton balerawland · water · seed · fertiliser · diesel · labour220 kg lint, 100 kg seed
cotton yarnprocesscotton bale · electricity · workersspool of yarn
dyed fabricprocessyarn · dye · water · electricity · effluent capturefabric · effluent (must equal input mass)
finished shirtprocessfabric · thread · packaging · cutting · sewingshirt · scrap (recyclable / landfill)
retail shirtdeliveredfinished shirt · ship-km · truck-km · store-energydelivered shirt with full label

each row is auditable. each handover is a signed claim. anyone can re-derive the carbon and energy totals from the graph. when a node lies, every downstream node either notices or has signed off on the lie. that is exactly the property the swarm enforces on this website's own knowledge graph (see /swarm) — applied to atoms instead of paragraphs.

does the market price the right thing?

today: mostly no. in textbooks the first welfare theorem proves a competitive market is efficient — but only when prices incorporate every cost. they don't. carbon, water, attention, social trust are all unpriced or radically under-priced.

what would it take to fix that without a central planner? three measurable interventions, all already partially deployed somewhere:

  1. publish the energy-cost alongside the price tag. anyone can already estimate this; the cost of computing and printing it is trivial.
  2. publish the carbon-cost under ISO 14067 with a small audited margin of error. EU's CSRD already requires this for large firms; extend to consumer items.
  3. publish the dependency graph. the EU Digital Product Passport (regulation EU 2023/1542 for batteries; expanding 2027+) is exactly this; let it become the default for every category, not just regulated ones.

none of these abolish willingness to pay. they let willingness compete with information. you decide which signal to weight. the market then converges on something that tracks the world more honestly — because the inputs to the market are more honest.
