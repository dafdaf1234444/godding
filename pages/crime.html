godding / crime

crime · the cross-cultural list

what humans broadly agree is wrong.

pulled from law codes, scriptures, and modern criminal codes across cultures and centuries. the names change; the list barely moves.

the core list

here is the short list that nearly every legal and religious tradition, on every continent, has independently arrived at as wrong:

  1. killing the innocent. codified in hammurabi (~1750 BCE), the ten commandments, the qur'an, the hindu dharma śāstras, every modern criminal code.
  2. theft. universal. even in the smallest hunter-gatherer bands, taking another person's tools is a punishable offence.
  3. fraud / lying for gain. as old as written contracts. roman law had specific terms for it; islamic law calls it ghish; modern fraud statutes rebrand it.
  4. betraying trust. oath-breaking, treason, abandoning a vulnerable person who relied on you. across every culture the word is roughly translated as "treachery," and is often considered worse than common theft.
  5. harming children. universal. the punishment is usually higher than for the same act against an adult.
  6. rape. universally considered criminal in modern law. older codes are uneven, but the act itself has been condemned in every major moral tradition.
  7. extreme cruelty. torture, mutilation, gratuitous violence. distinct from killing — the wrongness is the suffering, not the death.

why it's the same list

these aren't arbitrary. they most break the rule that life learned in order to keep going: cooperate, mostly. if any of these becomes routine in a group, the group falls apart, so groups that didn't punish them disappeared. the list isn't moral software handed down from above. it's a survival filter that wrote itself.

what people get wrong about crime

  • most crime isn't dramatic. the typical real-world crime is theft, fraud, or assault — not what the news amplifies.
  • most criminals aren't strangers. in the majority of homicides and the vast majority of sexual assaults, the victim knew the offender.
  • certainty of being caught matters more than severity. doubling the sentence has small effects; doubling the probability of being caught has large ones.
  • poverty is the strongest single predictor. not the only one. but holding everything else constant, economic precarity dominates.
  • most people are not violent. a small minority commits most violent crime in any given population. true everywhere it's been measured.

the part that connects to the rest of the site

the universal list of crimes is the same list as the universal list of "sins" in religion. the same list shows up in things people privately admit to disliking in politics. same list because it has the same source: the cooperation rule that life uses to keep itself going.

you don't need a god to tell you killing innocents is wrong. you need a species that survived long enough to put the rule in writing. that's all a god has ever been, on this question.
