godding / commons

platforms · alternatives that don't extract

commons — the platforms had the right idea. then the margin ate it.

the first generation of platforms — Uber, Airbnb, Amazon, Booking, DoorDash — were built on two real insights: information sharing is cheap, and standardised matching beats negotiated matching. those insights are correct. what came next was the extraction stage: take a 25–35 % cut, lock in users, lock out competitors, push price discovery into a black box, and use the surplus to buy more distribution. this page lays out the alternative that already exists in fragments — and where godding sits inside it.

the failure mode in one paragraph

a healthy platform is a matchmaker: low-overhead, transparent, a small fee for keeping the lights on. an extractive platform is a tollbooth: it owns the road and raises the toll until just below the price of building a parallel road. both shapes serve users at first; only one keeps doing it after the network effects lock in. when a platform's take-rate drifts up while its product quality drifts down, you are watching the second shape eat the first.

real cooperative + commons-based alternatives

none of these are theoretical. each one is operating today, with users and revenue, on the platform-cooperativism model articulated by Trebor Scholz (New School, 2014). they replace shareholder-extraction with member-ownership. governance scales the same way ant trails do — the rule is short and local, the result is a working network.

where godding sits in this picture

godding's commitment. this site is part of the knowledge commons, in the same shape as Wikipedia. it does not run advertising. it does not sell user data. it does not gate any page behind payment. the swarm that maintains it (/swarm) runs on a public schedule, in a public repo, with every accepted edit logged in a public file. the dependency map (/depends), the likelihood graph (/graph), and the audit log (data/audit.json) are all readable in plain JSON, by anyone, at any time. knowledge is published, not sold. if the project ever needs money to run, that need will be written down on this page, with a number, before money is asked for. until then: free to read, free to fork, free to argue with.

the four operational moves the commons model makes

1 · share information openly, by default

every claim, every method, every dataset is published with an open licence (CC-BY for prose, MIT for code, CC0 for raw data where legal). the original platforms gave us the discovery model — they should not get to own the discoveries. share-by-default also means: the price + carbon labels on every item live in a registry anyone can query, the way nutrition labels work today.

2 · standardise the units the way the metric system standardised mass

the second insight platforms had — that standardised matching beats negotiated matching — is correct, and we should keep it. one ride = one unit of "passenger-km on a regulated vehicle"; one stay = one unit of "person-night in housing meeting code X." everything audited under ISO 14067 for carbon, ISO 14040 for life-cycle, EN 15804 for buildings. the units already exist. what's missing is the requirement to publish them next to the price.

3 · cap volatility on staples; let signals work where they help

the author's position, stated honestly: fixed prices on a small list of staples — drinking water, basic grain, vaccines, generic medicines, primary energy — are an achievable goal. they have been achieved historically (utility-style regulation in the US 1935-78; OPEC's reference price; EU CAP for grain) and they prevent the wealth-extraction failure mode that floats up around essentials. this is one of the load-bearing claims this site makes; corrections are welcome on /vote.

this is not a call to abolish prices. fluctuating prices remain a useful signal for non-essential goods. it is a call to recognise that the things you cannot say no to should not be priced like the things you can.

4 · track every claim with provenance

every statement on this site is accountable to a source, a time, and a name (where one is given). the dependency map shows which page rests on which. the changelog records every accepted edit with a UTC timestamp. this is the same model the EU Digital Product Passport tries to apply to physical goods. apply it to ideas first; physical goods will follow.

but doesn't a commons need money to run?

yes. eventually. wikipedia runs on ~$180 million / year (financial statements, 2023), about 0.05 % of what google ads earns in the same period. the working precedent: donations + grants from people who use the thing, plus a strict rule that the people running the system are paid salaries — not equity, not options, not exit. the platforms-as-tollbooth failure happens when a venture investor needs a 10× return; a commons cannot have one of those in the cap table by construction. support the development of knowledge by funding its maintenance, not its sale.
