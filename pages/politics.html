godding / politics

politics · the gap

where the public mouth and the private mind disagree.

a short list. not a side. each item is a place where what people will privately admit and what they'll publicly say differs in ways that quietly shape elections, policy, and conversation.

the gap is real and measured

on issues from gender to immigration to religion in schools, surveys consistently find a difference between what people say in public and what they say when they think nobody is watching. on lgbtq topics in particular, recent polling finds gaps of 5–25 points, with the largest gaps among political independents. the size of the gap is the data.

seven items worth pointing at

  1. nation states are godded too. a flag is a god. a constitution is scripture. a passport is a sacrament. say that about a religion you don't belong to and nobody flinches; say it about your own country and watch what happens. that flinch is the point.
  2. values often track money. if you predict a person's stance from wallet and neighbourhood, you'll outperform any prediction from stated values. values are real; they're often the paint, not the engine.
  3. "both sides" reporting protects the louder side. if one claim is correct and the other isn't, "balance" implicitly halves the gap. naming this isn't partisan — it's just describing the math.
  4. anonymous quotes are doing real work. "x privately worry about y" isn't lazy reporting; it's an admission that public discourse is, on important topics, not where the actual conversation happens.
  5. opinion is cheap; revealed preference is the real ballot. ask people to give up a right for a policy and many will, hypothetically. ask them to write a small cheque toward it and the answer flips.
  6. the most stable political force is fatigue. people accept flawed governance as long as the flaws are familiar and the alternative requires showing up to a meeting on a tuesday.
  7. most fights are downstream of one fact. the rich don't want to be taxed; the poor don't want to be ignored; the middle don't want to fall. most domestic political fights in a democracy are reskins of those three sentences.

the test you can run on yourself

pick a political belief you hold strongly. write down: what would change my mind? if you can't write a real answer, the belief is faith — which is fine, but you should know that's what it is. faith dressed up as analysis is most of bad politics.
